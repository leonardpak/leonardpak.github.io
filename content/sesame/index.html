Candy House: Sesame Smart Lock

iOS/Android mobile app designs and design strategy for a smart home device startup.

ContextFreelancing and consulting with the startup Candy House
RoleMobile product design, design strategy, visual design

A humble disclaimer

This startup client's limited budget and timeline necessitated an expedited design process. There are more idealized ways to do design work, but I like to adapt to the circumstances of my clients/stakeholders.

The context

A design colleague and I were approached for freelance design and consultation after the startup, Candy House, received seed funding. Within weeks, they planned making a product announcement for Sesame, a smart home security lock.

Candy House needed design assistance to transition from a working prototype to a marketable product on a tight deadline. How would we help them move forward on a limited budget and timeline?

The problem

Jeremy, Candy House's co-founder, demos the installation of a prototype smart lock for us. After syncing the lock to a prototype mobile app, the whole installation is completed in under a minute.

The prototype product and mobile app presented to us. Photo credit: Candy House

"Other companies' locks are too complicated," said Jeremy as he also showed us competitors' locks and their arduous installations. "Our product must stay simple."

In our initial meeting, we agreed that our primary responsibilities would include:

  1. Creating a brand identity embodying the company's values, differentiating it from competition.
  2. Designing the company's first website and mobile apps based on approved brand direction.
  3. Providing consultation for product design work to be done after completion of our contract.

The design process

Throughout the process, my design colleague and I held multiple stakeholder meetings. Our initial meetings focused on understanding the product's prospective users and the competitive landscape.

Understanding users and the competition

Candy House shared their existing research with us regarding user motivations and typical lock usage patterns. Given the constraints, competitive analysis and user sentiment analysis were the most feasible research approaches we do in addition to the existing research.

Screenshots of competitor apps. How would you lock or unlock these smart locks? Which locks are locked or unlocked? How complex does an app need to be for managing locks?

After studying competitor hardware, interfaces, and both professional and user reviews, it became clear certain quirks gave users frustration. In particular:

  1. The installation process for most smart locks were difficult
  2. The interaction to lock or unlock the smart locks could be unclear (!)
  3. The status of a lock being locked or unlocked could be unclear (!)
  4. The design architecture of the mobile apps could be confusing

Understanding the company's values to create a brand

Our next stakeholder meetings focused on understanding the company, their vision, and their goals. We conducted exercises to determine the company's values and aligned them toward an overall brand direction.

Early-stage mood board and logo designs. Work credit: Wan-Ting Huang, my design colleague

My design colleague was chiefly responsible for creation of the brand assets that followed our exercises. My role during this time was consulting on the practical ramifications of certain brand directions.

Based on the company values we identified, we steered Candy House toward a minimalist and technological brand direction, differentiating the company through playful, colorful aesthetic matching their company name.

Understanding hardware constraints and improving the industrial design

Next, we focused on understanding the product hardware prototype and its constraints. This was crucial to design an interface that matched hardware capabilities. We also provided design recommendations based on the new brand direction:

  1. Size and shape — ideal product shapes
  2. Material and color — according to context or user preference
  3. Ambiance and sound — how environmental context, sound, and lighting could enhance usage
  4. Tactile sensation — the importance of the grip and feel of the knob

The evolution of the industrial design based on our recommendations. Photo credit: Candy House

Designing the website and mobile apps

We then shifted focus toward designing the interfaces. My design partner colleague worked on the company's marketing site, while I focused on designing iOS and Android mobile apps.

Early visual mockup for the website. Work credit: Wan-Ting Huang, my design colleague

My goal for the mobile app was to mitigate the issues users had with competitor apps while aligning with hardware capabilities. I explored various lock interaction, lock status, and mobile architecture approaches through sketches.

Over time, I collaborated with Candy House's developers to create iterations of screen flows until we came to a solution that worked within hardware constraints.

Mapping out a design strategy moving forward

With the branding, website design, and mobile app designs completed, our remaining work focused on ongoing design support and product design strategy moving forward. This included working on a marketing campaign with Sandwich:

Our recommendations for the product design strategy moving forward revolved around anticipated design needs for:

  1. Additional touchpoints and user scenarios
  2. Additional mobile and wearable platforms
  3. Utilization of new/upcoming mobile functionality and APIs
  4. Post-launch user and market research opportunities
  5. Refined animations and interactions

The results (and reflections)

The marketing website launched alongside a Kickstarter campaign that hit its $100,000 funding target within 9 hours. The mobile apps launched alongside initial product shipments.

Reviews spoke well of the design of the hardware, packaging, and mobile app, being described as "simple", "straightforward", "smooth" and "easy to use". Most importantly, the interaction for and the status of the lock wasn't confusing. A competitor also changed its mobile app architecture to be nearly identical to ours.

This client engagement was an interesting challenge in making the right compromises to best deal with constraints (as expected with an early-stage startup). Since then, we've continued occasional client work with Candy House, who went on to be a successful smart device company based in Tokyo, Japan.

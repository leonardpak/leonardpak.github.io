Candy House: Sesame Smart Lock

iOS/Android mobile app design and consultation for a smart home device startup.

ContextLate-2014 to early-2015, freelancing/consulting the startup Candy House
RoleMobile interaction design, design strategy, visual design

A humble disclaimer

The client's budget and timing necessitated an expedited and at times unideal design process done under tight constraints.

The context

My design partner and I were approached for freelance design and consultation soon after a startup, Candy House, had received their seed funding. Within weeks, they would be making a product announcement for Sesame, a smart home security lock.

Candy House needed critical design assistance to transition from working prototype to marketable product on a tight deadline. How would we help Candy House move forward to full-fledged product on limited budget and time?

The problem

Jeremy, Candy House's co-founder, is demoing the installation of a prototype smart lock for us. After syncing the lock to a crude mobile app, the whole installation is done in under a minute.

The prototype and early functional mobile app presented to us. Photo credit: Candy House

"Other companies' locks are too complicated," says Jeremy as he shows us competitors and their arduous installations. "Our product needs to stay simple."

During our initial meeting, we came to an agreement that our primary responsibilities would include:

  1. Creating a brand identity embodying the company's values while differentiating it from the competition.
  2. Designing the company's first website and mobile apps based on the approved brand direction.
  3. Providing design consultation and strategy for work to be done after completion of our contract.

The design process

Throughout the process, my design partner and I held many stakeholder meetings. Our initial meetings and work focused on understanding the product's users and the competitive landscape.

Understanding the users and the competition

Candy House was able to share their existing research with us regarding user motivations and typical lock usage patterns. Given the constraints, competitive analysis and user sentiment analysis were the most feasible research approaches we could add to the existing research.

Screenshots of competitor apps. How would you lock or unlock these smart locks? Which locks are locked or unlocked? How complex of an architecture is needed for managing locks?

After studying competitor hardware, competitor interfaces, and both professional and user reviews, it became clear certain quirks gave users frustration. In particular:

  1. The installation processes were difficult for most smart locks
  2. The interaction of locking or unlocking locks could be unclear (!!!)
  3. The status of a lock being locked or unlocked could be unclear (!!!)
  4. The architecture or layouts of the mobile apps could be confusing

Understanding the company to create a brand

Our next stakeholder meetings and work focused on understanding the company, their vision, and their goals. In doing so, we conducted exercises to assess the company's values to guide them toward an overall brand direction.

Early-stage mood board and logos designs. Work credit: Wan-Ting Huang

My design partner was chiefly responsible for the brand assets that followed our exercises, creating mood boards and presenting different brand approaches. During this time I advised on the ramifications of certain brand directions.

Based on the company values we identified, we steered Candy House toward a minimalist and technological brand direction, differentiating the company through a playful, colorful aesthetic.

Understanding hardware constraints and improving the hardware design

Next, we focused on understanding the product hardware and its constraints. This was crucial to make an interface design that matched the capabilities of the hardware. Furthermore, based on the new brand direction, we provided design recommendations for:

  1. Size and shape — ideal product dimensions and shapes
  2. Material and color — materials and colors appropriate to certain contexts or user preferences
  3. Ambiance and sound — how environmental context, sound, and light could impact usage
  4. Tactile response — the significance of the grip and feel of the knob

The evolution of the industrial design made use of some of our recommendations. Photo credit: Candy House

Designing the website and mobile apps

We then shifted our focus toward designing the software interfaces. My design partner worked on designing the company's marketing website, while I focused on designing iOS and Android mobile apps.

Early visual mockup for the website. Work credit: Wan-Ting Huang

My goal for the mobile app was to mitigate the issues users had with competitor apps while aligning with the hardware's capabilities. I explored various lock interaction, lock status, and mobile architecture approaches through sketches.

Early sketches of lock interactions, lock statuses, and app architecture concepts. Work credit: Leonard Pak

Over time, I worked with Candy House's remote developers to create iterations of wireframes and screen flows until we came to a solution that worked within the constraints of the hardware.

A later iteration of screen flows and wireframes for the iOS mobile application. Work credit: Leonard Pak

Following the approval of the wireframes, I created an interactive prototype to further communicate the overall flow and feel of the mobile design.

An example from the interactive prototype of the near-final design, showing the main interactions and overall architecture of the application. Work credit: Leonard Pak

With product launch only weeks away and budget constraints, I provided guidelines for transitioning the iOS mobile app design to an Android mobile app design. This helped save time and budget versus creating a whole new set of Android-specific deliverables.

Documentation explaining navigation and interaction differences between the iOS and Android designs. Work credit: Leonard Pak

Following completion of the mobile design, my design partner and I created visual mockups for the mobile application design, with my design partner creating all of the illustrations.

Visual mockups for the mobile application. Work credit: Wan-Ting Huang and Leonard Pak

Mapping out a design strategy moving forward

With the branding, website design, and mobile app designs completed, our remaining work focused on ongoing design support and design strategy moving forward. This included providing support for a marketing campaign created by Sandwich Video:

Our recommendations for the design moving forward revolved around considerations and anticipated design needs for:

  1. Additional touchpoints, circumstances, or contexts
  2. Additional mobile and wearable platforms
  3. New mobile functionality and APIs
  4. Post-launch user and market research
  5. Further refined animation and interaction work

The results (and reflections)

The website launched alongside a Kickstarter campaign that hit its $100,000 funding target within 9 hours. The mobile apps launched alongside initial product shipments at the end of 2015.

Reviews have spoken well of the design of the hardware, the packaging, and the mobile app. The mobile app has been described as "simple", "straightforward", "smooth" and "easy to use". Most importantly, the interaction for and status of the lock was never confusing. A competitor also changed its mobile app architecture to be similar.

This project was an interesting challenge. Making the right compromises to best deal with constraints was definitely the theme of this project (as expected with an early-stage startup).

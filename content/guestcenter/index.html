OpenTable: Guest Center Platform

Design leadership for the B2B side of the largest restaurant reservation service in North America.

ContextWorking at corporate in-house at OpenTable (owned by Priceline Group)
RoleWeb product design, prototyping, user research, visual design, design leadership

The context

OpenTable is the top restaurant reservation service in North America. While most OpenTable users book reservations, restaurants use OpenTable to manage reservations.

For most of OpenTable's history, reservations were managed through a Windows desktop application called the Electronic Reservation Book (ERB). The design and technology didn't age well.

Screenshot of OpenTable's legacy Electronic Reservation Book (ERB) software, showing a restaurant floor plan.

Later, OpenTable launched Guest Center for iPad, a more modern take on managing reservations, with features like cloud-based reservation data storage, multi-device support, etc.

Screenshot of OpenTable's modern Guest Center application, showing a restaurant floor plan and reservation information.

I joined OpenTable's restaurant product team as a lead product designer during this initial launch period. My contributions helped customers transition to the new product and built the foundation for a responsive web version of the product.

The problem

While new customers loved Guest Center, old customers hesitated to transition. Guest Center's initial release focused on establishing an initial product foundation, but was missing certain functionality important to certain restaurants.

How would the team bridge the gap, while keeping a user-centered approach, without repeating the mistakes of the past?

The design process

The following are examples of features I worked on to help fill in missing functionality.

Feature: tagging guests and reservations

Many classier restaurants take notes on guests and their visits in order to provide better service. Tagging helps quickly record and display various notes.

Excerpt from a desigm prototype for a restaurant host to select tags on a reservation's details page. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

Excerpt from a design prototype for a restaurant manager to add new tags for restaurant hosts to use. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

Guest Center has different interfaces for restaurant hosts (front-of-house, or FOH) and restaurant managers (back-of-house, or BOH), so I created designs for both interfaces for each feature.

I also conducted research with restaurants and OpenTable staff (who would install Guest Center for restaurants) to iterate on designs over time.

Feature: securing Guest Center with a passcode

Guest Center could put much power into the hands of restaurant employees. For example, restaurant hosts could choose to overbook a restaurant or block incoming reservations. A security passcode was needed to avoid misuse.

My work on this feature illustrates my typical design process well:

  1. Stakeholder interviews and reviewing existing research
  2. Listing out primary and fringe use case scenarios
  3. Sketching potential flowcharts for each use case scenario
  4. Sketching potential screens for each flowchart step
  5. Receiving initial feedback and iterating on the sketches
  6. Wireframing and/or mocking up interface screens
  7. Receiving further feedback and iterating on the wireframes/mockups
  8. Prototyping (and animating) the design
  9. Usability testing and iterating upon the design

A flowchart with sketched screens for each flowchart step, and a final screen flow of mockups (split into two images) for enabling the passcode usage in Guest Center. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

Along with documentation explaining the design (as necessary), I specified how the designs should be componentized for the web application and illustrated examples of how the final design should look.

Feature: providing a monthly overview of reservations

A legacy OpenTable product allowed casual restaurants to view a monthly overview of reservations, which Guest Center had unfortunately lacked.

A screenshot of a legacy product's monthly overview, which allowed hovering over dates and looking at icons to understand reservation activity.

To better understand why casual restaurants depended on it, I went to interview multiple users of the legacy product in casual restaurants at San Francisco.

Through the research, we came away with some insights which can't be shared publicly. However, some specific takeaways were that:

  1. Casual restaurants didn't typically look at reservations in the past, unless checking for records.
  2. Casual restaurants wanted easy awareness of shifts or days that were booked full, blocked from incoming reservations, or closed.
  3. Casual restaurants wanted an easy way to glance shifts or days that weren't empty anymore.

Per this research, I designed multiple iterations incorporating the feedback and later usability tested to validate the results.

Different iterations of the new month view, from early iteration to final iteration. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

I also provided technical details for how to build the month view responsively.

Documentation detailing the responsive behaviors of the month view's calendar, day cells, and content. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

The results (and some reflection)

Guest Center has continued to get more customers through fleshing out capabilities and working toward a self-service model (see the self-service signup wireframes excerpt below for another design example).

OpenTable was a good learning experience for me to better understand the challenges of transitioning from legacy softwares. During my time at OpenTable, I also took on some design team leadership responsibilities focused on team logistics and collaborating with a remote team. OpenTable was also one of my best experiences working collaboratively with product managers and engineers.

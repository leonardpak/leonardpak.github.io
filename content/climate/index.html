The Climate Corporation: Climate.com

Full-scale product re-architecture at an "ag-tech" (agriculture technology) data analytics startup.

Context2013 to 2014, working in-house at the startup The Climate Corporation
RoleWeb/mobile interaction design, information architecture, prototyping, visual design, user research, design strategy

A humble disclaimer

The Climate Corporation was acquired in late-2013 for $1.3 billion dollars. Pivots have altered the trajectory of the work shown here. "Climate.com" has also been rebranded as "FieldView".

The context

The Climate Corporation originally provided weather insurance to the agriculture industry. In late-2012, they launched Climate.com as a supplementary service to help farmers monitor their fields.

Screenshot of Climate.com from late-2012, showing weather data for a farmer's fields.

Climate.com garnered attention for it's big data approach to helping farmers plan their fieldwork. It quickly became apparent that it could go beyond the reporting of data and into the realm of providing actionable recommendations.

The problem

By early-2013, Climate.com gained popularity with early users who had constantly requested expanding the service. What was once supplementary was becoming its own full-blown product.

"We have 17 new features planned," said Powell, Climate.com's product manager. "And we have the thumbs up to move forward!"

After looking at the feature list, my jaw dropped. These weren't merely new features — the scale of the product was increasing tenfold.

These weren't merely new features — the scale of the product was increasing tenfold.

"All we have to do is add these to Climate.com," said Powell. "It'll be awesome! Now where to start...?"

The awkward silence that filled the room that moment confirmed my suspicions. We had a long few months ahead of us.

The design process

The full design process led to a re-architecture of Climate.com. Before delving into that, let's look at Climate.com mobile, as it heavily influenced the re-architecture project.

Mobile exposed Climate.com's potential

Increased interest was enough to consider creating a mobile version of Climate.com. I created several different iterations of prototypes, ranging from paper to high-fidelity, for research to assess the use cases for a potential mobile app.

Paper, mid-fidelity, and high-fidelity prototypes used to gather feedback. Work credit: Leonard Pak

The prototypes acted as talking points in our research for us to understand the goals users had with the data we had. Up to that point, Climate.com had only provided raw data.

The prototypes acted as talking points in our research for us to understand the goals users had with the data we had.

The research helped orient the design toward making the product user goal-oriented versus data-oriented, which set the tone for the re-architecture of Climate.com. The research also green-lit development of iOS and Android mobile apps.

Wireframes from initial mobile app design. Work credit: Leonard Pak

Starting the Climate.com Re-architecture

As I worked on adding new functionality to Climate.com, I questioned the assumption that the new features should be added to the original design. What good would an exorbitant amount of features be if the foundation was flawed? It was recipe for a bloated, not well thought-out product.

What good would an exorbitant amount of features be if the foundation was flawed?

I insisted on taking a step back to see how Climate.com could be holistically designed, accounting for features both new and old.

My approach encompassed:

  1. Using existing and gathering additional research to understand users' primary use cases
  2. Identifying flow and architecture inefficiencies based on the identified use cases in relation to the existing design
  3. Designing a new architecture, incorporating all current and proposed features, with efficient interaction flows based on the identified use cases

Using research to understand primary use cases

The first priority was to gather research to understand the main scenarios in which users would use Climate.com.

Because most research gathered to that point had been with existing customers, I used Ethn.io to reach out to prospective users to build a more complete understanding of the problem.

A research participant screener via ethn.io. Work credit: Leonard Pak

As a result, we gathered more diverse research via ethnography, interviewing, and surveys to better understand our users.

Analyzing the current design from the user's perspective

After identifying the primary usage scenarios from research, I compiled a set of user flows to analyze the existing design.

Analysis of a common usage scenario for Climate.com's existing design. Work credit: Leonard Pak

If we were to "simply add features" to the existing product, it would be adding to an already unideal, inefficient experience. Rebuilding the architecture would be key in creating a better user experience.

Designing a new product architecture

With an understanding of the architectural issues of the existing design, along with a list of planned features from the product roadmap, I designed a new architectural framework.

To achieve this end, I used an affinity diagram of the various features to determine potential architecture schemes that would fulfill users' primary use cases efficiently.

Affinity diagram of the final proposed architecture, with primary use cases in red text and architecture in green text, and a diagram of the new architecture. Work credit: Leonard Pak

After building the new architecture, I created a low-fidelity prototype, user flows, and design mockups based off of it.

A excerpt from the low-fidelity prototype, a user flow of the new architecture, and a mockup of the design. Work credit: Leonard Pak

This foundation would later be tested, refined, and established to set the tone of the product for the years to come.

The results (and some reflection)

Climate.com became successful, launching many of its planned features and accumulating over 50 million farm acres of fields.

The promise of Climate.com led to The Climate Corporation being acquired for $1.3 billion in late-2013. In 2015, all other products were sold off to focus solely on Climate.com as the primary product.

A screenshot of Climate.com PRO from 2015.

As of 2015, the re-architecture and design work I accomplished shows itself in the product. By taking a systematic design approach, product and design needs were anticipated months (and in some cases, years) in advance.

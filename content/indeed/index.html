Indeed: Internationalization

Hybrid design management/lead designer work for internationalization of the largest job search site in North America.

ContextWorking at corporate in-house in Tokyo, Japan offices
RoleProduct design, design localization, design management

The context

Indeed is the largest job search site in North America, and has been rapidly expanding internationally.

Initially, my role at Indeed encompassed both design managerial responsibilities and lead design work for desktop/mobile web, email, and chat services. In the following org chart, you can see my managerial people scope:

On the righthand side, my direct reports are in dark blue, and I also collaborated closely with other design employees in light blue. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

Eventually, as we entered a post-COVID world which changed the way worked, I transitioned to a full-time lead design work role, as it better suited me.

The problem

In joining the internationalization team of Indeed, a design team had already been established, but had lacked experienced leadership guidance.

As a hybrid design manager/individual contributor hire, how would I help a team of mostly mid-level and junior designers come together?

The management/design process

In my initial years, I focused primarily on management of the young design team.

Setting up better team foundations

For setting up better team foundations, I focused on a few key areas:

  1. Operations: running design reviews, handling performance management and hiring
  2. Research: collaboration with research vendors, arranging quicker feedback cycles
  3. Project management: managing a kanban board for design tasks, running planning/retrospectives and backlog management
  4. Collaboration/delivery: improving handoffs and design file management

I surmised that these types of improvements were key to fitting in within Indeed's process-driven, developer-centric culture.

Engaging within the company's design community

Besides focusing on my team, I also spent time engaging with the company's greater design community within Tokyo and internationally. This included hosting UX events in Tokyo, disseminating design education materials, creating better collaborative spaces in Tokyo, and helping hire for other teams in Tokyo.

Running collaborative design workshops

Indeed has a deeply rooted culture of doing collaborative exercises. Therefore another one of my key responsibilities was running collaborative design workshops. I was happy to leverage my extensive experience of running workshops from my design education days. I frequently ran collaborative workshops both in-person and remotely.

Examples of artifacts from both in-person and remote collaborative design workshops. Above are images of empathy map exercises done with groups in-person. Below is an image of brainstorming sessions done with groups remotely. Work credit: Leonard "Leo" Pak

The goals of these workshops were often to:

  1. Create clarity and alignment between functions on the problem, goals, and purpose of what is being accomplished
  2. Identifying potential issues, approaches, requirements, risks, and concepts
  3. Helping plan and prioritize in coming quarters/years
